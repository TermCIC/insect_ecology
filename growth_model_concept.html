Introduction to Growth Models

by Dr. Chun-I Chiu

Department of Entomology and Plant Pathology, Chiang Mai University

What are Growth Models?

Growth models are mathematical representations used to describe how populations or organisms grow over time under various conditions. They help in understanding biological, ecological, and agricultural processes.

Types of Growth Models

1. Linear Growth Model

In this model, the population increases by a fixed amount in each time step. Growth is constant over time and does not depend on the current population size.

Formula: \( N(t) = N_0 + r \cdot t \)

2. Exponential Growth Model

This model describes a situation where the population grows proportionally to its current size. It leads to rapid growth over time.

Formula: \( N(t) = N_0 \cdot e^{r \cdot t} \)

3. Logistic Growth Model

In this model, the population grows rapidly at first but slows down as it approaches the carrying capacity, which is the maximum population size the environment can sustain.

Formula: \( N(t) = \frac{K}{1 + \frac{K - N_0}{N_0} \cdot e^{-r \cdot t}} \)

  • Carrying Capacity (K): The maximum population size that can be supported by the environment (used in the logistic model).
  • Time (t): The time elapsed.

Applications of Growth Models

  • Ecology: Predicting population dynamics and understanding species interactions.
  • Agriculture: Modeling crop growth and pest population dynamics.
  • Medicine: Simulating the spread of diseases or the growth of cancer cells.
  • Environmental Science: Assessing the impact of resource limitations on population growth.

Comparison of Growth Models

  • Linear Growth: Simplest model with a constant growth rate.
  • Exponential Growth: Describes rapid, unchecked growth; applicable to ideal conditions.
  • Logistic Growth: Realistic model accounting for environmental limits and resource availability.

Visualizing Growth

Graphs can help illustrate how populations change over time under different growth models. For instance:

  • A linear graph shows a straight line.
  • An exponential graph curves upwards steeply.
  • A logistic graph forms an S-shaped curve, plateauing at the carrying capacity.
