Spatial Quantitative Methods

Types of Traps

Malaise Trap

The Malaise trap is a tent-like structure designed to capture flying insects, particularly those actively moving within an area. The insects hit the vertical barrier and are funneled into a collection bottle containing a preservative.

Example: A Malaise trap placed in a forest understory to collect mosquitoes and flies for biodiversity surveys.

Interception Trap

Interception traps are vertical sheets or nets that intercept insects in flight. These traps are effective in areas where insects travel along specific corridors, such as near tree lines or above ground-level vegetation.

Example: Interception traps set up in a mangrove forest to study beetles or wasps moving between trees.

Pitfall Trap

The pitfall trap is a simple method for capturing ground-dwelling insects and arthropods. A container is buried flush with the ground surface and filled with a preservative or bait. Insects fall into the trap and are unable to escape.

Example: Pitfall traps installed along a transect in a grassland to collect ants, spiders, or beetles.

Pros

  • Quantifies spatial diversity effectively.
  • Captures all insects entering the trap space, ensuring random sampling.
  • Allows for targeted sampling in specific areas of interest.

Cons

  • Limited catch size, especially in low-density insect populations.
  • Small spatial coverage, requiring multiple traps for broader studies.
  • Traps may bias collection based on insect size, behavior, or movement patterns.
