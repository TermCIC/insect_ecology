Test your knowledge about p-values and comparisons of normal distributions!

Quiz

1. What does a p-value represent?

  1. The probability that the null hypothesis is true.
  2. The probability of obtaining results as extreme as the observed ones, assuming the null hypothesis is true.
  3. The probability of a significant difference between two distributions.
  4. The overlap between two distributions.

2. What condition makes two distributions more likely to have a significant difference?

  1. Small difference in means and large standard deviations.
  2. Large difference in means and small standard deviations.
  3. Both distributions having large sample sizes.
  4. Both distributions having small sample sizes.

3. How does increasing the sample size affect the p-value?

  1. The p-value increases due to more data overlap.
  2. The p-value decreases due to increased statistical power.
  3. The p-value stays constant regardless of sample size.
  4. The p-value becomes invalid for large samples.

  1. The sample sizes are not specified.
  2. The standard deviations are equal.
  3. The means are far apart relative to the standard deviations.
  4. The null hypothesis is true.

5. If two distributions have the same mean but different standard deviations, how does this affect their overlap?

  1. The overlap decreases.
  2. The overlap increases.
  3. The overlap remains unchanged.
  4. The p-value is invalid in this scenario.

6. Why is the difference in means important for determining significance?

  1. It directly reduces the overlap between distributions.
  2. It increases the overlap between distributions.
  3. It does not affect the p-value.
  4. It only matters for large sample sizes.

7. Which of the following leads to a smaller p-value?

  1. Large standard deviations and small mean difference.
  2. Large mean difference and small standard deviations.
  3. Small sample sizes.
  4. Both distributions having identical parameters.

8. What happens when two distributions have no overlap?

  1. The p-value is close to 1.
  2. The p-value is exactly 0.
  3. The p-value is undefined.
  4. The p-value is very small.

9. If one distribution has a much larger standard deviation than the other, what is the likely impact on p-value?

  1. The p-value increases due to greater overlap.
  2. The p-value decreases due to smaller overlap.
  3. The p-value becomes invalid.
  4. The p-value is unaffected.

10. Which of the following combinations is most likely to result in a significant p-value?

  1. Small difference in means and large standard deviations.
  2. Large difference in means and small standard deviations.
  3. Identical distributions with small sample sizes.
  4. Distributions with random parameters.
